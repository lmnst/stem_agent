# stem-agent: write-up

> Every number in this document comes from the deterministic
> primitive-search path. There is no LLM in the system. Section 7
> describes the LLM extension point as a design note, not as a
> stubbed second path.

## 1. Problem framing

The problem class is **single-function Python bug repair**. A task is
a folder with `solution.py` (one buggy function, ~5 to 25 lines) and
`test_solution.py` (pytest-compatible asserts). A task is **solved**
when every `test_*` callable returns without raising, run inside an
isolated temp workspace.

Why this class fits the brief:

- **Narrow enough that "specialized" is concrete.** A specialized
  agent here means *which AST mutations to try first, with what
  budget per task, when to stop, and which workflow steps to run.*
  Those choices are measurable.
- **Automatically verified.** No graders, no judging models; a
  subprocess runs the test file and exits 0 or non-zero.
- **Has recurring families.** The in-bank benchmark has eight bug
  classes; a generic agent has no reason to prefer one over another;
  a specialized agent can.

## 2. What the stem starts as

The stem is **not** an undifferentiated agent. It is a domain-specific
Python AST repair agent that ships with:

- the eight primitives in `stem_agent/primitives.py`
  (`swap_compare_strict`, `flip_compare`, `swap_eq_neq`,
  `shift_const_pm1`, `swap_arith_pair`, `swap_and_or`,
  `swap_true_false`, `swap_call_args`);
- the runner / sandbox / variant-dedup machinery;
- a workflow vocabulary of `run_tests`, `propose`, `apply_check`.

What the stem does *not* know is **which primitive to prefer for
which task**, how generous a budget the domain warrants, when to give
up, and how task features interact with primitive choice. Those are
the things evolution discovers. The stem's primitive priority is
`sorted(PRIMITIVE_NAMES)` (alphabetical, no curation), its budget is
8, and its `policy_weights` field is empty.

## 3. Headline: budget-controlled comparison on the in-bank test split

The four-row table is the headline. It is generated by
`python -m stem_agent.cli compare ...` from the persisted blueprints.
Wilson 95% intervals are on every pass rate. *iters/all* counts
unsolved tasks at their effective per-task budget so a worse
blueprint cannot flatter its mean by averaging only the easy solves;
*iters/solved* is the classical metric. Both are reported.

| row                    | budget | pass            | CI95            | iters/solved | iters/all | wall_s |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| stem (default)         |   8    |  9/12 = 75.0%   | [46.8, 91.1]    |        2.56  |     3.92  |  0.95  |
| stem (evolved budget)  |  12    | 11/12 = 91.7%   | [64.6, 98.5]    |        3.91  |     4.58  |  1.04  |
| evolved (stem budget)  |   8    | 11/12 = 91.7%   | [64.6, 98.5]    |        3.00  |     3.42  |  0.82  |
| evolved (default)      |  12    | 12/12 = 100.0%  | [75.8, 100.0]   |        3.50  |     3.50  |  0.84  |

**What this says.** At matched budget the evolved beats the stem on
both rows: at b=8 the gap is 9/12 vs 11/12, and at b=12 the gap is
11/12 vs 12/12. The headline is no longer "75 -> 100"; it is "75 vs
91.7 at b=8" plus "91.7 vs 100 at b=12." The Wilson intervals overlap,
so on n=12 this is a suggestive single-run result rather than a
statistically separable one. The previous draft's "75 -> 100" framing
mixed priority and budget effects; the controlled view splits them.

The evolved is also faster: across the whole split it spends about
0.13s less wall time per task than the stem at the same budget,
because (a) the per-task ordering hits the right primitive earlier
and (b) the policy's low-confidence detection caps the budget on at
least one task that would otherwise burn through.

## 4. The blueprint diff and the learned artifact

Field-level diff between `stem_blueprint.json` and
`evolved_blueprint.json`:

| field | stem | evolved | source of change |
|---|---|---|---|
| `primitive_priority` | alphabetical | reverse-alphabetical | dev-set selection |
| `primitive_budget`   | 8            | 12                   | `recommended_budget` from train iters |
| `early_stop_no_progress` | 8        | 12                   | tied to budget |
| **`policy_weights`** | **`{}`**     | **8 primitives x 14 features (30 positive weights)** | **fit on train+dev observations** |
| `policy_confidence_threshold` | 0.0 | 3.317                | 25th percentile of solved-task max-scores |
| `policy_fallback_budget` | 0        | 2                    | median train+dev iters-to-solve |

`policy_weights` is the learned, per-task artifact the system
discovers. It is absent from the stem, derived entirely from train
and dev solve observations, serialized in the blueprint's JSON, used
at runtime by `agent.solve_task` to reorder the variant queue per
task, and pinned by tests
(`tests/test_policy.py`,
`tests/test_pipeline.py:test_evolved_carries_a_learned_artifact_absent_from_stem`).

How weights are derived (`stem_agent/policy.py:fit_policy`): for each
primitive `p` and each feature `f`, the weight is the mean of `f`
across solved tasks where `p` was the fixer, minus the mean of `f`
across all solved tasks. So the weight is positive when `f` is
characteristic of `p`'s solve context (and negative when `f` is
anti-characteristic). The agent at runtime computes the per-primitive
score `sum_f features[f] * weights[p][f]`; the highest-scoring
primitive's variants are tried first, with ties broken by the global
priority. When the top score is below
`policy_confidence_threshold`, the agent runs `policy_fallback_budget`
attempts instead of the full budget and notes `policy: low
confidence`.

The threshold and the fallback budget are themselves data-derived:
the threshold is the 25th percentile of max-primitive-score on solved
train+dev tasks (so by construction it sits below most in-bank
tasks), and the fallback budget is the median observed iters-to-solve
(so giving up still gives the easy fixes a couple of chances).

This artifact is what makes the evolved differ from the stem on more
than ordering and budget. With it, **per-task runtime behaviour
depends on task features**, not just on a global priority.

## 5. Out-of-bank challenge split

The challenge split (`benchmarks/pybugs/challenge/`) is eight
hand-authored tasks whose bugs are deliberately *not* single-site
applications of any existing primitive. They were written from the
bug-class side first; the primitive bank was consulted afterward to
verify out-of-bank-ness. The eight classes are: wrong identifier,
constant by |delta| > 1, multi-edit, missing branch, wrong slice,
wrong builtin, wrong literal type, multi-site arithmetic.

| row                    | budget | pass        | CI95        | iters/all | wall_s |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| stem (default)         |   8    | 0/8 = 0.0%  | [0.0, 32.4] |     8.00  |  0.91  |
| stem (evolved budget)  |  12    | 0/8 = 0.0%  | [0.0, 32.4] |    12.00  |  1.01  |
| evolved (stem budget)  |   8    | 0/8 = 0.0%  | [0.0, 32.4] |     6.50  |  0.82  |
| evolved (default)      |  12    | 0/8 = 0.0%  | [0.0, 32.4] |     9.50  |  0.91  |

Both the stem and the evolved fail every challenge task; the bank
cannot reach these fixes by single-site mutation. The difference is
in *how* they fail. The stem burns its full budget on every task. The
evolved hits the policy's low-confidence threshold on `ch_001`
(wrong-identifier) and `ch_004` (missing-branch), reducing its budget
to 2 on those tasks and emitting `policy: low confidence; budget
exhausted`. Across all eight tasks the evolved spends 1.5 fewer
iterations per task at b=8 and 2.5 fewer at b=12. That is a clean,
principled give-up: not a fix, but a measurable cost reduction
attributable to the learned policy.

The remaining six challenge tasks score above the policy's threshold,
because their feature counts (an arith op, a comparator, etc.) match
patterns the policy saw in train+dev, so the policy thinks they are
in-bank. That is the sparse-data limitation, reported plainly.

## 6. What evolution discovered, what it merely confirmed

**Discovered (would not have written in advance).**
- The per-task primitive ranking the policy fit derives is
  non-trivial and surprised me. For example, `swap_arith_pair`
  receives a *negative* weight on `n_int_const` (-0.5) because the
  arith-fixed train tasks tended to have *fewer* int constants than
  the average solved task. So a buggy source with many constants
  *and* arith ops is steered away from `swap_arith_pair` and toward
  primitives whose typical context had constants (`shift_const_pm1`,
  `flip_compare`). I would not have authored this; it is a learned
  property of the train+dev distribution.
- Reverse-alphabetical priority dominates ranked-by-frequency on
  dev. The reason is not "popularity"; it is variant fan-out. The
  primitives at the head of `sorted(reversed)` (`swap_true_false`,
  `swap_eq_neq`) generate few variants on a typical source, so they
  fall through cheaply and let `swap_compare_strict` catch the bug
  in 1-2 attempts. The popular primitives (`shift_const_pm1`,
  `swap_arith_pair`) generate the most variants, so putting them
  first burns the budget before the next primitive runs.
- The 25th-percentile-of-max-score threshold lands in a useful
  place: it correctly identifies `ch_001` and `ch_004` as
  out-of-bank without flagging any test-split solve as
  out-of-bank.

**Merely confirmed (would have written in advance).**
- Bigger budgets help: `recommended_budget = max(8, max_iters * 1.5
  + 4)` from the train probe is a fine heuristic and reproduces.
- A primitive's typical solve context has at least one feature with
  positive lift on that primitive's "natural" structural element
  (compare ops on `swap_compare_strict`, arith ops on
  `swap_arith_pair`, etc.). I expected this; the policy reproduces
  it.

## 7. Limitations and decisions

- **No LLM in the headline path.** The previous version of the
  system carried an LLM proposal path (a single-shot model reply
  prepended to the variant queue). It was real code, with tests,
  but its contribution to the headline numbers was zero (the demo
  ran without an API key). To avoid an unmeasured second path, the
  LLM machinery is removed. A natural future extension would slot
  in at the `propose` workflow step: prepend a model-generated
  variant to the queue and let the policy and the budget machinery
  rank it. That is described, not stubbed.
- **Sparse training data.** With 12 train solves plus 8 dev solves,
  the per-(primitive, feature) weight estimates are noisy.
  `swap_eq_neq` is fixed in *no* train+dev task (the equality
  family appears only in the test split, by deliberate split
  design), so its row in `policy_weights` is all zeros. The policy
  learns nothing about it. With more data this would correct.
- **The policy can hurt at budgets that are tighter than the dev
  winner expects.** At `b=8` the evolved is 11/12, not 12/12: the
  policy reorders variants for task_024 (`signs_match`) in a way
  that pushes the actual fixing primitive (`swap_and_or`) past the
  budget cap. At `b=12` (the policy's expected budget) this does
  not happen. This is an honest cost: per-task ordering trades
  worst-case in-bank robustness for out-of-bank give-up speed.
- **The benchmark is small (40 tasks total) and authored.** The bug
  families are realistic but I did not draw from a public bug
  corpus. Results are not claims about general program-repair
  performance.
- **The primitive set is closed.** Eight AST mutations cover the
  in-bank families and nothing else. Out-of-bank tasks fail. The
  policy makes the failure cheaper but does not fix anything.

## 8. Where to look in the code

- Stem and the evolved-blueprint construction:
  `stem_agent/evolve.py:stem_blueprint`, `evolve`
- Domain analysis: `stem_agent/analysis.py:analyze_domain`
- Per-task policy fit: `stem_agent/policy.py:fit_policy`,
  `fit_threshold`, `fit_fallback_budget_from_iters`
- Agent loop using the policy at runtime:
  `stem_agent/agent.py:_resolve_policy`, `solve_task`
- Wilson 95% CI helper: `stem_agent/stats.py`
- Budget-controlled comparison: `stem_agent/cli.py:cmd_compare`
- Mutation primitives: `stem_agent/primitives.py`
- Sandboxed runner: `stem_agent/runner.py`

The pipeline writes the following under `artifacts/`:

- `profile.json` -- the `DomainProfile` from train probing.
- `stem_blueprint.json` -- domain-agnostic baseline blueprint.
- `evolved_blueprint.json` -- dev-set winner with fitted
  `policy_weights`, `policy_confidence_threshold`, and
  `policy_fallback_budget`.
- `evolution_log.json` -- per-generation, per-candidate scoring
  trace with full blueprint, parent, mutation reason, per-task
  records, and the final stop condition.
- `stem_test.json`, `evolved_test.json` -- per-task evaluation on
  the held-out test split.
- `compare_test.json` -- the four-row in-bank table.
- `compare_challenge.json` -- the four-row challenge-split table.
